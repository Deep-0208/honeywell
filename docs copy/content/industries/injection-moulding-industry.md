# 3. Injection Moulding Hydraulic Architecture

A standard hydraulic injection moulding machine contains several distinct hydraulic sub-circuits, each with specific performance requirements:

*   **Clamping Circuit:** Generates the massive clamping tonnage that holds the mould halves together during injection. This circuit must achieve full clamping force rapidly and hold it with zero drift during the injection and cooling phases.
*   **Injection Circuit:** Drives the injection screw/ram forward to inject molten plastic into the mould cavity. This circuit requires precise proportional velocity control during injection to prevent shear-related surface defects (jetting, weld lines).
*   **Ejection Circuit:** Drives the ejector plate to push moulded parts out of the mould. Typically a simple on/off circuit requiring a fast, clean stroke.
*   **Core-Pull Circuits:** Drive side cores or collapsing cores to create undercut features in the moulded part. Core-pulls must complete their sequences (pull-then-mould or mould-then-pull) within the overall cycle time and must be individually load-held to prevent core movement during injection.
*   **Screw Drive Circuit:** Powers the hydraulic motor that rotates the injection screw during plasticizing (recovery phase).

# 4. Core-Pull Hydraulic System Design

The hydraulic core-pull system is where Honeywell Hydraulics adds the greatest value to injection mould builders. Core pulls are typically:

- Mounted directly on the mould base, severely limiting space
- Required to complete extension or retraction within 0.5–2 seconds
- Required to hold position under injection pressure without drift (load-holding)

Our [Custom Manifold Blocks](/manifold-blocks/custom-manifold-blocks/) are specifically designed for in-mould core-pull applications. We machine compact blocks integrating directional valves, sequence valves, and pilot-operated check valves (POCVs) into a single block that bolts directly to the mould base — eliminating external piping and providing guaranteed load-holding capability.

# 10. FAQ

**Q: Our core-pull cylinders keep drifting during the injection phase. How do we fix this?**
**A:** Core drift during injection is caused by injection pressure acting on the core face, which creates a hydraulic back-pressure that overcomes an inadequately specified load-holding valve. The fix is to install a properly pilot-operated check valve (POCV) directly at the cylinder's A port — not at the manifold inlet. The POCV must be pilot-operated from the main system pressure to ensure it remains firmly locked during injection.
