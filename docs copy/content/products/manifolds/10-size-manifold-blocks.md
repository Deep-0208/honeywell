# 3. When Is NG16 (10-Size) Required?

The most common and costly mistake in hydraulic manifold specification is **undersizing the valve interface**. Machine builders frequently default to NG10 (CETOP 5) blocks because they are cheaper and more readily available — then wonder why their machines run hot, cycle slowly, and suffer premature valve failure.

**The rule is simple:** If your circuit flow rate exceeds 80 LPM at the directional valve, you must use NG16 or larger. Common scenarios requiring NG16:

*   **Large-bore cylinders:** A 160mm bore cylinder extending at 0.1 m/s requires ~200 LPM. Routing 200 LPM through an NG10 block creates 15+ m/s fluid velocity — this is catastrophic for the valve and the system.
*   **Multiple small cylinders on one valve:** If one directional valve serves two or three cylinders simultaneously, the combined flow demand may exceed NG10 capacity.
*   **High-speed automation:** Presses and handling equipment requiring rapid cylinder travel speeds generate high peak flow demands that exhaust NG10 capacity.
*   **Long-stroke cylinders:** Large fluid volume requirements per cycle may not cause velocity problems but create temperature issues in NG10 blocks with their smaller T gallery cross-sections.

# 5. NG16 Flow Velocity Validation

Our engineers always validate that the selected block size maintains safe internal gallery velocities before machining. Here is the validation logic for NG16:

**Target maximum internal velocity:** 4.5 m/s (conservative limit for long-term reliability)
**Standard NG16 P/T gallery bore:** 22mm
**Maximum safe flow at 4.5 m/s:** Q = A × v = (π × 0.022² / 4) × 4.5 × 60,000 ≈ 205 LPM

This confirms that the NG16 standard gallery diameter handles up to 200 LPM continuously within a safe velocity margin. For circuits demanding 200–250 LPM peak flow, we increase the gallery bore to 25mm in our heavy-duty variants.

NG16 blocks are predominantly used in heavy industrial environments where material selection is critical:

| Operating Condition | Recommended Material | Pressure Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |

| Heavy press rooms, heat | ST52 Carbon Steel (zinc plated) | 315 Bar |
| Forge shops, high vibration | Ductile Iron (GGG50, epoxy coated) | 315 Bar |
| Marine/outdoor high-flow | SS316L (electroless nickel) | 250 Bar |

*Ductile Iron is frequently specified for NG16 blocks in forging and heavy-press environments because its damping characteristics absorb the pressure pulsations generated by large displacement gear pumps, extending valve service life.*

# 12. FAQ

**Q: How do I know if I need NG10 or NG16?**
**A:** Calculate your maximum circuit flow: bore (m)² × π/4 × velocity (m/s) × 60,000 = LPM. If this exceeds 80 LPM, specify NG16. If it exceeds 200 LPM, specify CETOP 8 (NG22).

**Q: Can I mount an NG10 (CETOP 5) valve on an NG16 (CETOP 7) block?**
**A:** No. The bolt patterns, port locations, and O-ring sizes are completely different between sizes. Each CETOP size is a distinct, non-interchangeable interface.

**Q: Do you stock standard NG16 single-station subplates for quick delivery?**
**A:** We maintain buffer stock of standard NG16 single-station subplates in both aluminium and steel. Standard configurations (G3/4" BSPP ports) can typically be despatched within 3–5 working days.

**Q: What is ductile iron and why use it for NG16 blocks?**
**A:** Ductile iron (GGG50) is a cast iron alloy with superior mechanical toughness and excellent vibration damping compared to steel. For high-flow circuits driven by gear pumps (which produce significant pressure pulsation), ductile iron blocks absorb the pulsation energy, reducing stress fatigue on valve body seals and extending the overall system service life.
